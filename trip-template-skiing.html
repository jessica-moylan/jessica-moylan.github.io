Utah Wasatch Backcountry

Five Days in Little Cottonwood Canyon

📅 February 12-16, 2026 📍 Alta, Utah ❄️ 32" fresh snow

Skinning up toward the ridgeline above Grizzly Gulch

Trip Statistics

📅 5 days Duration
📈 ~25,000 ft Vertical Feet
❄️ 32" New Snow
⚠️ Moderate Avy Danger

Overview

Five days exploring the legendary powder of Utah's Wasatch Range, based out of Alta. A storm cycle brought over 30 inches of new snow on a stable base, creating near-perfect backcountry conditions. Daily avalanche forecasts from the Utah Avalanche Center guided our terrain choices and kept us safe while we found incredible lines.

Day 1: Grizzly Gulch

Started with a classic—Grizzly Gulch from the top of Little Cottonwood Canyon. The skin track was well-established, winding up through dense pine forest. After about 90 minutes and 2,000 feet of climbing, we reached the ridge with views into both Cardiff Fork and Mineral Fork.

Conditions were stellar—12 inches of fresh on a stable base, minimal wind effect. We dropped into a west-facing bowl and linked turns through untracked powder for over 1,500 vertical feet. The snow quality lived up to the hype—light, dry, and bottomless.

Day 2: Cardiff Fork

Another storm overnight brought 8 inches of fresh. We targeted Cardiff Fork, a popular zone with multiple descent options. The approach is straightforward—a well-packed skin track up the valley floor.

We skied multiple laps off the main ridge, finding fresh lines each time. The highlight was a northeast-facing chute that held perfect cold smoke. Three hours of climbing, four descents, and about 5,000 feet of vertical. Tough to beat.

Day 3: Rest and Reconnaissance

With continued snowfall and rising avalanche danger (Moderate to Considerable), we took a rest day. Spent the morning checking gear, studying the forecast, and planning safer objectives for the remaining days.

In the afternoon, we did a mellow tour up Albion Basin to scout lines for the next day. The snow continued to pile up—another 6 inches overnight.

Day 4: Albion Basin

With the avalanche danger dropping back to Moderate and good bonding between the new and old snow, we returned to Albion Basin for a full day. Skinned up to Sugarloaf Pass, then worked our way across the ridge linking multiple bowls.

The coverage was incredible—you could ski anywhere. We found wind-loaded pockets on leeward aspects and untracked powder in the trees. Six hours on the mountain, probably 6,000 feet of vertical.

Day 5: Mineral Fork

Final day. Legs were tired but we couldn't resist one more mission. Mineral Fork offered a longer approach with less traffic. Skinned for about 2 hours to reach a high ridge separating Mineral and Cardiff.

The descent was worth every step of the approach—2,500 feet of perfect turns through a wide-open bowl. We ended with one last lap down a mellow tree run, finishing with huge smiles and worked legs.

Gear Notes

  • Skis: 108mm underfoot all-mountain touring skis—perfect width for these conditions
  • Skins: Pomoca Climb 2.0—great grip on steeper sections
  • Beacon: BCA Tracker 4—practiced searches daily
  • Pack: 30L with probe, shovel, first aid, layers, and food
  • Layers: Started cold each morning, stripped down to base layer while skinning

Safety Considerations

We checked the Utah Avalanche Center forecast every morning and evening. With Moderate danger, we stuck to lower-angle terrain (below 35°) and avoided steep, wind-loaded slopes. We also practiced beacon searches and discussed group management protocols before each tour.

Having partners with formal avalanche training made decision-making easier and more confident. When conditions were questionable, we chose conservative terrain or took a rest day.

Lessons Learned

  • Utah powder lives up to the hype, but proper avalanche education is non-negotiable
  • Starting early (7 AM skin tracks) meant fresh lines and better snow stability
  • Hydration is critical at altitude—we went through 2-3 liters per day
  • Rest days are important for both safety and enjoying the full trip
  • The Wasatch is popular—popular zones can have dozens of parties on busy days

Resources

Avalanche Forecast: Utah Avalanche Center
Maps: CalTopo and Gaia GPS
Weather: Mountain-Forecast.com for hourly conditions
Permits: None required for backcountry access
